What Is Spearfishing?

Spearfishing is one of the oldest forms of fishing practiced by humans — predating rods, nets, and boats by thousands of years. It requires a hunter to freedive, holding their breath and descending into the ocean to pursue and selectively harvest individual fish with a spear or speargun.

Unlike conventional fishing, spearfishing produces virtually zero by-catch. You see exactly what you target before you shoot. This selectivity makes it one of the most sustainable and ethical ways to harvest wild seafood. I've been spearfishing since my teenage years off the coast of Southern California, and the sport has taken me from shallow reef dives to open-water blue water hunts — including landing a 50-pound yellowfin tuna on a breath-hold dive.

The underwater world that spearfishers navigate — vibrant, silent, and demanding precision. Photo: Unsplash

Every dive is a physical and mental challenge. You must control your breathing, equalize ear pressure as you descend, manage buoyancy, and remain completely calm while hunting — all on a single breath. The sport demands physical fitness, patience, and a deep understanding of marine ecosystems.

Essential Gear Guide

Having the right equipment makes the difference between a safe, successful dive and a dangerous one. Here's everything you need, from entry-level to advanced:

  1. Freediving & Safety Equipment
    • Low-volume freediving mask (less air = easier pressure equalization)
    • Long freediving fins — fiberglass or carbon fiber blades for efficiency
    • 3mm–7mm wetsuit for thermal protection and neutral buoyancy
    • Weight belt with lead weights to achieve negative buoyancy at depth
    • Dive knife strapped to the leg for entanglement emergencies
    • Dive flag & float — legally required in most US coastal states
  2. Spearguns & Pole Spears
    • Pole spear — beginner-friendly, silent, great for reef fish
    • Rubber-band speargun — the most common choice; 60cm–120cm depending on fish size
    • Pneumatic speargun — compressed air-powered, excellent power for big game
    • Roller speargun — advanced design; maximum power for open-water pelagics like yellowfin
  3. Rigging & Accessories
    • Spear shaft with flopper tip (prevents fish from sliding off)
    • Reel or breakaway line system for large fish
    • Dive light for hunting in caves, overhangs, and at depth
    • Gloves and booties for cold water and coral protection
  4. Catch & Handling Gear
    • Stringer or mesh catch bag clipped to the float line
    • Large cooler packed with ice — always ready on the boat
    • Fillet knife and cutting board for processing fish on the water
"When I hunted that 50-pound yellowfin, I was using a 110cm rubber-band speargun with a 7mm shaft and a flopper tip connected to a 25-meter reel. The fish ran hard — the reel screamed — and the fight lasted almost ten minutes before I could bring it to the surface." — Tanner Petty

Techniques & Strategy

Spearfishing is as much a mental game as it is a physical one. Different environments call for completely different hunting strategies, and experienced spearfishers develop an instinct for reading water conditions, fish behavior, and depth.

Reef Hunting — The most common technique. You find structure: rocks, kelp beds, reefs, ledges. You descend, lay still near the bottom, and let the fish approach you. Camouflage wetsuits help. Patience wins.

Blue Water Hunting — The most adrenaline-charged discipline. You drop into open, featureless ocean and wait in the water column for pelagic species — yellowfin tuna, wahoo, mahi-mahi — to investigate you out of curiosity. Depths of 30–50 feet are common. This is where I took my biggest fish.

Drift Diving — You drop into a current and allow it to carry you along reef walls and drop-offs while you hunt. Covers a lot of ground with minimal effort. Ideal for areas with consistent currents.

The 50-Pound Yellowfin Story

Yellowfin tuna are a legendary target in the spearfishing world. They're fast, wary, and unpredictable. On a blue water dive off the California coast, I descended to 30 feet and went motionless, hanging in the blue water column. A school of yellowfin materialized from the deep — curious about the silhouette above them. The shot had to be perfect: gill plate, lateral line. One breath. One chance. The fish ran with an explosion of power that ripped the reel almost empty. I surfaced with a 50-pound yellowfin and the memory of a lifetime.
